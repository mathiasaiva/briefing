U.S. Strikes More Than 80 Sites in Iran and Chokes Off Its Oil Exports

Iran / Strait of Hormuz

U.S. Central Command hit more than 80 targets across Iran on July 7, striking air defense systems, command and control networks, coastal radar and anti-ship missile sites, along with more than 60 Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps small boats. The barrage followed Iranian attacks on commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, which Washington called a violation of the June ceasefire. Explosions were reported on Kharg Island, the offshore terminal that handles roughly 90 percent of Iran's crude exports, as well as at Bandar Abbas, Sirik and Qeshm. In the same move Washington revoked the waiver that had let Tehran sell oil, leaving millions of barrels without a buyer. Iran, now led by Ali Khamenei's son Mojtaba since his father was killed in a February strike, warned of a "crushing response."

80+Iranian sites struck
~90%of Iran crude via Kharg
60+IRGC boats hit

How it works: Washington is running two levers at once. The military strikes degrade Iran's ability to threaten shipping, while pulling the oil waiver strangles the revenue that funds those forces. Kharg is the single point of failure: cripple one small island and most of Iran's export earnings stop, which is why Tehran treats any threat to it as an act of economic war.

Why it matters
This is the fragile February ceasefire breaking down in slow motion, and it radiates into the rest of the briefing. The same war is draining the global stock of Patriot interceptors that Ukraine needs (see the Kyiv story below), and the threat to Hormuz is what keeps a floor under oil prices even as markets otherwise sit at records.

Russia's Heaviest Barrage in Weeks Kills at Least 22 Around Kyiv

Ukraine

Russia launched 351 drones and 68 missiles at Ukraine overnight into July 6, killing at least 22 people, most of them in and around Kyiv. Fifteen died in the capital itself and 56 were injured. Ukrainian officials said all 29 ballistic missiles fired reached their targets, a near total failure of interception that came days after a separate strike killed 31 people in Kyiv, the deadliest single attack on the city this year.

351drones launched
68missiles fired
22+people killed

How it works: Ukraine can shoot down slow drones and cruise missiles with a mix of systems, but ballistic missiles fall so fast that only the U.S.-made Patriot can reliably stop them. The war in Iran has strained the global supply of Patriot interceptors, so Ukraine is rationing a weapon it cannot replace fast enough, and Russia is deliberately loading its salvos with ballistics to exploit that gap.

Why it matters
The rising death toll is a direct consequence of a supply chain problem created by a different war. It also sharpens the argument at the NATO summit in Ankara (see Politics), where continued support for Ukraine, and who pays for the interceptors, is one of the three headline agenda items.

Hamas Dissolves Its Gaza Government After Nearly Two Decades

Gaza

Hamas announced on July 6 that it is dissolving the government it has run in Gaza for close to twenty years and handing authority to the National Committee for the Administration of Gaza, a technocratic body created in January under UN Security Council Resolution 2803 and the U.S.-backed 20-point peace plan. The transfer is still theoretical: Israel has not yet allowed the committee's members, led by acting commissioner Ali Abdel Hamid Shaath, to enter the Strip. The move comes with the October ceasefire largely stalled and more than 1,060 Palestinians reported killed since it took effect.

How it works: By giving up formal governance to neutral technocrats, Hamas is trying to remove Israel's stated reason for continued operations and to appeal directly to Washington, positioning itself as the side willing to let Gaza be rebuilt. The catch is that a handover only counts if Israel lets the new administrators physically govern.

Why it matters
It is a bet that political theater can restart a peace process that fighting has frozen. Whether Israel admits the committee will show if the 20-point plan is still alive or has quietly collapsed, a question that shapes the whole regional picture alongside the Iran war.

Attacks on Tankers Keep the Strait of Hormuz on a Knife's Edge

Persian Gulf / Global Shipping

A fully laden LNG carrier owned by a Qatari state shipping company was struck by a projectile near the Omani coast while exiting the Strait of Hormuz, the latest in a run of attacks on vessels transiting the waterway. Roughly 20 percent of the world's oil, and a large share of its liquefied natural gas, passes through this narrow channel. Brent crude settled about 3 percent higher at $74.16 a barrel on the news, reversing a slide that had briefly taken prices below $70 as traffic recovered in late June.

It is also the pretext behind the U.S. strikes and the oil sanctions in the lead story, and a recurring theme at the NATO talks.

Congo Starts First Drug Trial Against a Rare Ebola Strain as Deaths Pass 500

Democratic Republic of Congo

The World Health Organization and Congo's national biomedical research institute began enrolling patients on July 2 in a clinical trial testing three treatments against the Bundibugyo strain of Ebola, a species with no approved vaccine or specific therapy. The drugs under study are the antiviral remdesivir and two monoclonal antibody therapies, MBP-134 and maftivimab. As of July 4, the outbreak in the DRC had reached 1,561 confirmed cases with 506 deaths and 254 recoveries.

1,561confirmed cases
506deaths
3therapies in trial

How it works: The vaccines and antibody drugs stockpiled from earlier epidemics were built for the more common Zaire strain and do not clearly work against Bundibugyo, which is why a fatality rate this high has no off-the-shelf answer. Running a trial mid-outbreak lets doctors treat patients and gather evidence at the same time, the only realistic way to develop a therapy for a virus that appears too rarely to study in calm conditions.

Why it matters
A strain-specific treatment would turn a near untreatable outbreak into a manageable one and build a template for the next rare-pathogen emergency. It is a reminder that public health capacity, not just military or economic power, is a form of national resilience.

NATO Meets in Ankara to Turn a Spending Pledge Into Signed Contracts

Ankara, Turkey

Leaders of NATO's 32 members gathered in Ankara on July 7 and 8 for a summit built around defense spending. Secretary General Mark Rutte wants allies to lay out concrete plans to hit the new target of 5 percent of GDP on defense and security by 2035, split into 3.5 percent for core military needs and 1.5 percent for related security spending. The alliance unveiled billions of dollars in new arms projects, including a deal to replace its aging surveillance aircraft, to show President Trump the pledge is becoming real. Trump kept up the pressure even after landing, criticizing allies for what he called weak support in the U.S. war with Iran.

5%of GDP target by 2035
32member states
3.5%for core defense

How it works: Last year's summit won the promise; this year is about enforcement. Rutte's tactic is to convert a percentage on paper into procurement contracts that are politically hard to cancel, locking in the spending before any future government can walk it back.

Why it matters
The summit is where three threads in this briefing meet: sustaining Ukraine's air defense, sharing the cost of the Iran war, and deciding whether Europe can defend itself with less American help. Trump's complaint about allied support for the Iran campaign signals that U.S. backing for NATO is increasingly conditional on reciprocity.

Platner says he is weighing "the best path forward." Under Maine rules, July 13 is the deadline for him to withdraw and be replaced, roughly four months before Election Day.

Trump Revokes Iran's Oil Waiver, Turning Sanctions Into a Weapon of War

Washington

Alongside the July 7 airstrikes, the Trump administration revoked the waiver that had allowed Iran to sell crude, reimposing sanctions in direct retaliation for the tanker attacks in the Strait of Hormuz. The decision stranded millions of barrels of Iranian oil with no clear buyer. The move landed as Trump was meeting allies at the NATO summit, where the security of Hormuz sat high on the agenda.

Millionsof barrels stranded
Waiverrevoked in retaliation

How it works: A sanctions waiver is a switch, not a slow policy. Flipping it off instantly bars banks and buyers worldwide from touching Iranian crude, so the White House can inflict economic pain the same afternoon as a military strike, without waiting for a new law or a UN vote.

Why it matters
It shows how tightly military and economic tools are now fused in the Iran campaign. The same decision that punishes Tehran also tightens global oil supply, which is why it shows up again in the Economics section as pressure on prices and, through energy costs, on inflation.

Congress Clears the Last of the 2026 Funding Bills, With a Catch

United States / Capitol Hill

The House passed the Senate's version of the Consolidated Appropriations Act by 217 to 214, completing all twelve fiscal year 2026 spending bills after Trump signed the package into law. At the president's direction, the deal folds in a two-week continuing resolution for the Department of Homeland Security, covering border and immigration agencies, FEMA, the Coast Guard, the Secret Service and the cyber agency CISA, buying time for negotiations over that final piece.

Democrats Pitch an Agenda Aimed at the "Annoyance Economy"

United States

Searching for a domestic message that cuts through a foreign-policy-dominated news cycle, Democrats are assembling a governing blueprint that targets what one proposal calls the "annoyance economy," the robocalls, endless hold times, hidden fees and other everyday frustrations that voters share regardless of party. The push is an attempt to reclaim a pocketbook narrative while the party manages the fallout from the Maine Senate crisis.

How it works: Kitchen-table irritations poll well because almost everyone experiences them, so they let a party talk about consumer costs without wading into the more divisive fights over the economy or the wars abroad. The bet is that specific, tangible grievances travel further than abstract slogans.

Why it matters
With midterms roughly four months out and international crises crowding the headlines, both parties are hunting for a domestic story voters actually feel. Whether "everyday annoyances" can compete with war and markets for attention is a real test of message discipline.

Hiring Nearly Stalls in June Even as Unemployment Ticks Down

United States / Labor Market

The U.S. economy added just 57,000 jobs in June, well below the roughly 115,000 economists expected and down from a downwardly revised 129,000 in May. The unemployment rate still fell, to 4.2 percent from 4.3 percent. Leisure and hospitality shed 61,000 jobs on weak seasonal hiring, while professional services, social assistance and healthcare added the most. April and May payrolls were revised down by a combined 74,000. Wages rose 0.3 percent on the month and 3.5 percent over the year.

57,000jobs added
4.2%unemployment rate
61.5%participation, 4-yr low

Read past the headline: A falling jobless rate normally signals strength, but this one is weak. The rate dropped because the labor force participation rate fell 0.3 points to 61.5 percent, its lowest since 2021. In plain terms, unemployment did not fall because more people found work; it fell because people stopped looking and dropped out of the count. Fewer job seekers shrinks the denominator and flatters the number.

Why it matters
A cooling labor market would normally push the Fed toward cutting rates, but with inflation still elevated the central bank is stuck watching prices instead (see the next card). Slow hiring plus sticky inflation is the uncomfortable mix policymakers least want to face.

The Fed Holds Rates as Inflation Keeps It From Easing

United States / Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve left its benchmark rate at 3.5 to 3.75 percent at its June meeting and is widely expected to stay on hold when the FOMC next meets on July 28 and 29. Officials say inflation remains elevated relative to the 2 percent goal, driven in part by energy and other supply shocks. Under new Chairman Kevin Warsh, seen as more hawkish than his predecessor, some policymakers are even keeping the door open to rate hikes later this year.

3.5-3.75%fed funds range
2%inflation target
Jul 28-29next decision

How it works: The Fed has one main tool and two conflicting problems. It would normally cut rates to support a weakening job market, but cutting also stokes inflation, which is still too high partly because of the energy prices tied to the Iran war. So it does nothing, a "pause" that is really a bet that prices cool before hiring cracks.

Why it matters
Every part of this briefing runs through the Fed. Oil pressure from Hormuz feeds inflation, which keeps rates high, which raises the hurdle for the AI-driven stock rally and for borrowers everywhere. A hawkish Warsh means less rescue if markets wobble.

AI Chip Stocks Wobble as Investors Question the Payoff

Global Markets / Semiconductors

After a record second quarter, chipmakers stumbled in early July as doubts crept in about whether enormous AI spending will earn its keep. In one session Micron fell about 13 percent, wiping out roughly $138 billion in market value, while Intel and AMD dropped around 9 and 7 percent and the VanEck Semiconductor ETF shed 5 percent after a 71 percent second-quarter surge (single-source figures). Nvidia slipped below a $5 trillion valuation. Triggers included reports that SK Hynix is slowing high-bandwidth memory expansion, that China's DeepSeek is developing its own AI chip, and the Fed's hawkish turn under Warsh.

How it works: AI hardware stocks priced in near-flawless future demand. Any hint that customers might build their own chips (DeepSeek), that memory suppliers see softening orders (SK Hynix), or that higher-for-longer rates make far-off profits worth less today (the Fed) knocks out a leg of that story, and richly valued stocks fall hardest when the story cracks.

Why it matters
AI investment has done much of the heavy lifting for the whole market this year, so a semiconductor wobble is a stress test for the broader rally in the next card. If the AI trade stalls, the record-high indexes lose their main engine.

Oil Climbs Back Toward $74 as Hormuz Risk Refuses to Fade

Global Commodities

Brent crude settled about 3 percent higher at $74.16 a barrel after the strike on a Qatari LNG carrier and the U.S. decision to revoke Iran's oil waiver. Just days earlier prices had dipped below $70, near their lowest since late February, as tankers resumed transit through the Strait of Hormuz and OPEC+ signaled more supply. Brent had averaged $85 in June before easing.

$74.16Brent, up ~3%

<$70brief July low

How it works: Oil is trading a risk premium, not just barrels. When ships move freely and OPEC+ adds supply, prices sag toward fundamentals near $70; when a tanker is hit or sanctions bite, traders price in the chance of a bigger disruption and bid crude back up. The swings measure fear as much as physical shortage.

Why it matters
Energy is the transmission belt from the Iran war to your wallet. Higher oil feeds directly into the elevated inflation that is keeping the Fed on hold, which is why a single strike near Oman ripples all the way to U.S. interest rates.

United States / Equities

Despite war, sticky inflation and a chip scare, U.S. stocks finished the first half of 2026 near records and pushed higher into July, with the Dow topping 53,000 for the first time. Over the first six months the Dow rose 8.9 percent, the S&P 500 gained 9.6 percent, the Nasdaq climbed 12.8 percent, and the small-cap Russell 2000 jumped nearly 22 percent, its best first half since 1991. Estimated S&P 500 earnings growth for the second quarter stands at about 23 percent.
